RICHARD AVEDON

“Portraits are never copied. The moment that emotion or fact is transformed into a photograph is no longer a fact, but a point of view. The picture is not inaccurate. So all the photos are accurate, but none of them are true.”

Photoghotographer Richard Avedon (American, 1923–2004) is renowned for his striking, intimate, and dynamic portraits of prominent 20th-century figures, as well as images of mental patients, circus performers, and others living on the fringes of society.

He worked first as a photographer for the Merchant Marines, taking identification photos. He then moved to fashion, shooting for Harper's Bazaar and Vogue, demanding that his models convey emotion and movement, a departure from the norm of motionless fashion photography.

Richard Avedon is a different photographer: he started with fashion photography. So he has photographed many beauties and celebrities. But he didn't forget what he wanted to do because of his working relationship: man. Not only did he shoot models and celebrities, he was more of an ordinary person, a person he knew, and someone he met on the street. Not only did he work in the studio, but he also moved to the outdoors and political life, and he even took pictures on the streets. That's why Richard Avendon is a photographer of all kinds. But his most famous work is definitely his portrait.

Richard Avedon's style is very unique: his biggest characteristics is a pure white background(minority is gray), his subject is posed, placed in the front of the screen, the background is white or deep shallow ash. He never USES a natural light source or a shadow, and he tries to avoid a shadow on the background board when the flash hits. He never tried to hide the character's physical defects, wrinkles, eye bags, scar on the picture is very conspicuous, but not so attractive is the true real appearance, attracted the interest of the audience more than perfect.

Avedon's portraits are almost always big pictures. And he often has a little bit of exposure. The purpose of this is, in fact, the same as the pure white background: to reduce the distraction of unimportant things to the audience. And he does not exclude the shallow depth of the painting. In the absence of a background cloth, he often USES shallow depth of field to blur the background, or even the body of the subject. These methods and resolution of large format, there formed a very tension visual style: describes the subject to a great extent in the face of all the details at the same time, all other things are hidden. This allows the viewer to see the most subtle expressions and eyes of the subject. Everything is blown up.

His fashion photos are just the opposite: he likes to bring models and assistants outside the studio to shoot on the streets. And in his fashion photography, models are often in motion. In one sentence: the model is not a clotheshorse in his photo, but a living person.

If you want to use a word to describe Richard Avedon's style, it might be: direct. It's almost brutal. In front of his camera, people are stripped of all the masks they wear in everyday life and are forced to show their true colors. And that true face usually surprises us. Richard Avedon's photographs show so few things that they become more and more abundant.

Biography

1923 -

Born in New York, NY

1942-1944 -

Served in the U.S. Merchant Marines

1945 -

worked for Harper's bazaar

1946 -

Set up his own studio

1960 -

Start shooting fringe people, political events

1964 -

Published book 'Nothing Personal'

1985 -

Named Photographer of the Year, American Society of Magazine Photographers

1986-1987 -

Recipient, Certificate of Recognition, Visiting Artist, Harvard University

1989 -

Awarded Honorary Doctorate, Royal College of Art, London, England

1992 -

Became the first staff photographer for The New Yorker

1993 -

Recipient, Master of Photography Award, International Center of Photography

1993 -

Awarded Honorary Doctorate, Kenyon College

1994 -

'EVIDENCE' is awarded Prix Nadar by the Bibliotheque Nationale for 'Best Photographic Book'

1994 -

Awarded Honorary Doctorate, Parsons School of Design

1996 -

Recipient Humanitarian Award, Mental Health Association of New York City

1998 -

Recipient Award for Lifetime Achievement in the Arts, Alliance for Young Artists & Writers

2000 -

Awarded Photo District News: Most Influential Photographer of the Last Twenty Years

2001 -

Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences

2003 -

Kitty Carlisle Hart Award, Arts & Business Council, New York

2003 -

Recipient Royal Photographic Society, 150th Anniversary Medal

2003 -

Recipient Arts & Business Council, Award for Outstanding Achievement in the Visual Arts

2003 -

Recipient Americans for the Arts, National Arts Award: Lifetime Achievement

2004 -

Died in San Antonio, TX
